About Scott Sampson

Scott Sampson is a dinosaur paleontologist, science communicator, and passionate advocate for reimagining cities as places where people and nature thrive. He serves as president and CEO of Science World British Columbia, one of Canada's top science centers, and sits on the board of several organizations, including the Canadian Museum of Nature. He is also a popular speaker and author, known for his appearances on numerous television shows, including Dinosaur Train and Jurassic Park: Secrets Revealed.

Talks

  • The Power of Storytelling in Science Communication

    What do dinosaurs, climate change, and urban sustainability have in common? They all tell stories about our world. In this talk, Scott Sampson explores the importance of storytelling in science communication and shares tips for crafting engaging and effective narratives.

  • How Dinosaurs Can Help Us Understand Climate Change

    What can the ancient world of dinosaurs teach us about the challenges we face today? In this talk, Scott Sampson explains how studying the climate of the past can help us predict and prepare for the climate of the future.

    How can we create cities that are not only sustainable but also resilient in the face of environmental change? In this talk, Scott Sampson looks to the natural world for inspiration and shares insights from his work on urban ecology and green infrastructure.

Fossil Threads in the Web of Life

February
Scott Sampson

What's 75 million years old and brand spanking new? A teenage Utahceratops! Come to the Saroyan, armed with your best dinosaur roar, when Scott Sampson, Research Curator at the Utah Museum of Natural History, steps to the podium. Sampson's research has focused on the ecology and evolution of late Cretaceous dinosaurs and he has conducted fieldwork in a number of countries in Africa.

Scott Sampson is a Canadian-born paleontologist who received his Ph.D. in zoology from the University of Toronto. His doctoral work focused on two new species of ceratopsids (horned dinosaurs) from the Late Cretaceous of Montana, as well as the growth and function of certopsid horns and frills.

Following graduation in 1993, Sampson spent a year working at the American Museum of Natural History in New York City, followed by five years as assistant professor of anatomy at the New York College of Osteopathic Medicine on Long Island. He arrived at the University of Utah accepting a dual position as assistant professor in the Department of Geology and Geophysics and curator of vertebrate paleontology at the Utah Museum of Natural History. His research interests largely revolve around the phylogenetics, functional morphology, and evolution of Late Cretaceous dinosaurs.

In addition to his museum and laboratory-based studies, Sampson has conducted paleontological work in Zimbabwe, South Africa, and Madagascar, as well as the United States and Canada. He was also the on-air host for the Discovery Channel's Dinosaur Planet and recently completed a book, Dinosaur Odyssey: Fossil Threads in the Web of Life, which is one of the most comprehensive surveys of dinosaurs and their worlds to date.
